# Knowledge Base Analysis: Family Support Factor

**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Factor:** Family Support (6 items)
**Status:** Partially sufficient

---

## 1. Overall Sufficiency Verdict

**Partially sufficient.** The knowledge base provides a credible evidence anchor (Sheridan et al. meta-analysis) and identifies active ingredients that are relevant to the factor's construct space. However, it has three structural shortcomings that limit its utility for recommendation generation:

- **It operates primarily at the programmatic/systemic level** (CBC, family-school partnership programs), not at the level of individual teacher actions within a classroom.
- **It does not differentiate by severity or score level.** The same ingredients are offered regardless of whether a child shows no parental support or inconsistent support — yet the appropriate teacher response differs substantially.
- **It under-addresses the observational items** (15, 18, 23) where the teacher witnesses concerning parental behavior but is not the intervention agent. These are precisely the items most likely to produce low scores, and the knowledge base offers no guidance on the teacher's role as a reporter, documenter, or referral initiator.

## 3. Differentiation Analysis by Score Level

### Score 0 (Low) — Little or no observable parental support

This is the most critical gap. A low score likely indicates that the teacher observes signs of emotional harm, dismissal, or disengagement from parents. The knowledge base offers partnership-based interventions (CBC, joint goal-setting) that **presuppose parental willingness to engage** — an assumption that may not hold when the score is 0. The KB provides no guidance on:

- How a teacher should document and report concerns (items 18, 15)
- When to escalate to a school psychologist, social worker, or child protection services
- How to support the child directly when family-level intervention is not feasible or safe
- How to maintain a supportive teacher-child relationship as a compensatory buffer

At this level, the most important teacher actions are likely **observational, protective, and referral-oriented**, none of which appear in the current active ingredients.

### Score 1 (Medium) — Inconsistent or partial parental support

This is the level where the current knowledge base is most applicable. Structured communication routines (#4), joint goal-setting (#1), and cross-setting coordination (#2) are designed to strengthen an existing but unreliable home-school connection. The KB could support recommendations such as initiating regular check-ins or proposing shared behavioral goals. However:

- The KB does not help the teacher distinguish between parents who are willing but lack skills vs. parents who are intermittently available vs. parents who are supportive in some domains but not others.
- The active ingredients are described generically; the teacher would need significant adaptation guidance to move from "structured communication routines" to a concrete weekly action.

### Score 2 (High) — Strong, consistent parental support

At this level, the teacher's role shifts to **maintenance and reinforcement**. The KB does not address this scenario — it is implicitly oriented toward deficit remediation. There is no guidance on how to leverage strong family support as a resource (e.g., inviting the family to contribute to classroom SEL activities, using the family as a model for peer mentoring). The absence of maintenance-oriented recommendations means the system may generate generic or unnecessary suggestions for well-supported children.

## 4. Identified Gaps

1. **No coverage of teacher response to observed emotional maltreatment** (items 15, 18, 23). These items position the teacher as a witness to concerning parenting behavior, yet the KB offers only collaborative intervention models that assume willing, non-harmful parents.

2. **No referral pathways or escalation protocols.** For low-scoring children, the most appropriate teacher action may be to refer to a school psychologist or social worker. The KB is silent on this.

3. **No child-focused compensatory strategies.** When family support is low and unlikely to change in the short term, the teacher's most actionable role is strengthening the teacher-child relationship, building emotional safety in the classroom, and connecting the child to other supportive adults. None of this is present.

4. **No guidance on co-regulation or affect stabilization** (item 11). The mechanism by which parental interaction stabilizes a child's emotional state is a developmental construct (co-regulation) that is absent from the active ingredients.

5. **No differentiation framework by score level.** The KB provides a single list of ingredients without any severity-based logic for when each is appropriate.

6. **No cultural or contextual adaptation for Ukraine.** Ukrainian school structures (e.g., the role of the class leader / klasnyi kerivnyk, norms around parent-school communication, expectations about home visits) differ from the North American context of the cited evidence. The CBC model and Scottish toolkit may not transfer directly.

7. **No guidance on professional boundaries.** Parent coaching (#5) risks positioning the teacher as a parenting instructor, which may be inappropriate, unwelcome, or counterproductive — particularly in cultures with strong norms around family privacy.

8. **No maintenance or enrichment recommendations for high-scoring children.** The KB assumes a deficit model throughout.

9. **Item 11 is an outcome indicator, not a behavior.** The KB does not distinguish between items that describe parental actions (3, 5, 15, 18, 23) and items that describe child outcomes (11), yet these require different intervention logics.

---

## 5. Recommendations for Knowledge Base Improvement

1. **Add a tiered response framework** mapping score levels to categories of teacher action:
   - Score 0 → observe, document, refer, compensate (child-focused)
   - Score 1 → engage, communicate, coordinate (partnership-focused)
   - Score 2 → maintain, leverage, reinforce (strengths-based)

2. **Add content on teacher-as-observer and mandatory reporting/referral protocols.** Sources: OECD guidance on child protection in schools; Ukrainian legal framework on child welfare reporting (if available); the linked NC2S trauma-informed toolkit (which likely contains relevant material but is not extracted in the current entry).

3. **Add compensatory relationship strategies** for when family support is low. Key sources:
   - CASEL framework on teacher-student relationships as a protective factor
   - Hughes & Kwok (2007) on teacher-child relationship quality and at-risk students
   - Pianta's (1999) work on the teacher-child relationship as an intervention lever

4. **Add co-regulation content** for item 11. Source: Bath & Seita's work on the three pillars of resilience (safety, connection, coping); Stuart Shanker's Self-Reg framework.

5. **Add boundary-setting guidance** specifying what teachers should and should not do regarding family dynamics — including explicit statements about when to hand off to specialists.

6. **Extract and summarize actionable content from the linked resources.** The current entry lists three open resources but does not indicate what specific, teacher-usable strategies they contain. The NC2S toolkit and the Scottish engagement toolkit likely contain relevant material that should be parsed and mapped to the screener items.

7. **Add Ukrainian contextual notes:** the role of the klasnyi kerivnyk, norms around parent meetings, legal obligations, and culturally appropriate communication strategies. Consider consulting Ukrainian educational psychology literature or practitioner guides (e.g., from the New Ukrainian School reform materials).

8. **Separate parental-behavior items from child-outcome items** in the recommendation logic. For item 11 (affect stabilization), the teacher needs strategies for supporting emotional regulation in the classroom — not strategies for changing parental behavior.
